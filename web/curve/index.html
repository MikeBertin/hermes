The Elliptic Curve

Bitcoin's keys live on secp256k1, the curve y² = x³ + 7. Its points form a group: you can add two points with a ruler and you can multiply a point by a whole number. That multiplication, k·G, is a one-way street — easy forwards, hopeless backwards. That asymmetry is your private key → public key.

Things to notice

The vertical line is "infinity"

Add a point to its own mirror image and the line through them is vertical — it never hits the curve a third time. That missing point is the group's zero, the point at infinity.

Real intuition, integer reality

Bitcoin doesn't use the smooth curve — it uses points over a 256-bit prime, a cloud of ~10⁷⁷ dots. The algebra is identical; only the picture changes.

One generator, every key

On secp256k1, one fixed point G generates the whole group. Your private key is just how many times you added G to itself. Counting forward is instant; counting back is the discrete-log problem. (In this page's toy field the demo G traces out a small subgroup — big curves are chosen so that can't happen.)
